Who We Are

A family united in
faith and hospitality

The Little Sisters of the Poor is a Catholic Religious Order serving the needy elderly of 65 years and above, irrespective of nationality, tribe, race or creed. We are a nonprofit organisation depending wholly on Providence — and on the generosity of our benefactors — for the upkeep of those in our care.

The Congregation was founded by Saint Jeanne Jugan in 1839 in France. Today we serve on all five continents, in more than 30 countries. Here in Mombasa, we endeavour to live as a family according to the founding charism of our beloved foundress.

"Back in 1961, Bishop Eugene Butler CSSP invited the Little Sisters to open a home in his Diocese. On June 7th 1969, three sisters arrived in Kenya for the foundation."

— History of the Mombasa Home

The Sisters first rented a house on Kilindini Road before moving to the present address in Tudor, where a home with a capacity of 74 beds was built. For 56 years, hundreds of elderly people of all faiths, tribes, and nationalities have found a home here. The Jeanne Jugan family in Mombasa comprises the Little Sisters, the elderly, staff members, the Association Jeanne Jugan, benefactors, friends and volunteers from all walks of life.

Our Foundress

Saint Jeanne Jugan
Mother of the Poor

The life of a humble woman whose single act of charity gave birth to a worldwide mission of love for the elderly poor.

October 25, 1792 — August 29, 1879
Jeanne JuganSister Mary of the Cross

Born in Cancale, Brittany, France, Jeanne grew up in poverty after her father was lost at sea. From an early age she knew God had a special call for her — "a work not yet founded," she told her mother at 24 when she refused marriage.

On one winter's evening in 1839 in Saint Servan, she opened her home to an elderly blind woman who had found herself alone after the death of her sister. Jeanne carried her on her back to give her her own bed. From that single act of love, the Congregation of the Little Sisters of the Poor was born.

Despite an injustice that stripped her of her role as Superior — which she met only with silence and humility — Jeanne spent 27 years in quiet prayer at La Tour Saint-Joseph, forming the young Sisters in the original charism.

"Love God very much, and the Poor in Him."
"Take good care of the elderly, for it is Jesus himself whom you care for in them."
"Making the poor happy is all that counts."
"Treat the poor compassionately, and Jesus will treat you kindly on your last day."
"Jesus is waiting for you in the Chapel. Go find him when you are at the end of your patience."
July 13, 1879

Heroicity of Virtues Recognised

The Church officially acknowledged the heroic virtues of Jeanne Jugan, beginning her formal cause for sainthood.

October 3, 1982 — Pope John Paul II

Beatification

Before 6,000 pilgrims from around the world, Pope John Paul II declared Jeanne Jugan "Blessed" — calling her the most humble woman of Cancale, "so poor in possessions yet so rich in faith." He praised her prophetic intuition of the needs of the elderly: to be respected, loved, and to deepen their faith.

October 11, 2009 — Pope Benedict XVI

Canonisation

Pope Benedict XVI proclaimed Jeanne's holiness, calling her "a beacon to guide our societies." He honoured her "compassionate gaze drawn from profound communion with God" and her ever-timely charism for a world where so many elderly suffer poverty and solitude.

Novena Prayer to Saint Jeanne Jugan

Jesus, you rejoiced and praised your Father for having revealed to little ones the mysteries of the Kingdom of Heaven. We thank you for the graces granted to your humble servant, Saint Jeanne Jugan, to whom we confide our petitions. Father of the poor, hear the petitions she presents on our behalf, through Mary your Mother and ours. Amen.

Benefactor

Saint Jeanne Jugan used to remind the Little Sisters of their grave duty to pray daily for their benefactors. She would say: "What would we do without them."

The elderly we welcome are those who are financially poor, without anyone to provide their daily needs. Each day, two Little Sisters go out to ask for donations — in kind or in money — for their upkeep. Without your precious assistance, we could not make the last days of our elderly happy.

Donations can be monetary, in kind, or expertise — according to the capacity of each one. We are deeply grateful to each of you, and we pray for your intentions every day. In the Chapel, the elderly and the Sisters pray for the intentions of our Benefactors, Friends and family members.

Association Jeanne Jugan
& Volunteers

The Association Jeanne Jugan was established in 1998 as a contemporary response to the prophetic intuition of our foundress. She envisioned hospitality as a humble, charitable service uniting in one family the Little Sisters, the elderly, and lay collaborators who wish to serve Jesus Christ in the person of their needy brothers and sisters.

Since its approval by the Vatican, over 2,000 women and men around the world have joined the Association — from all walks of life and professions, drawn to the person of Saint Jeanne Jugan. To join, one must be at least 18 years of age and participate in a yearlong programme of formation. Membership is renewed each May 1st, the Feast of St. Joseph the Worker.

Activities you can be part of:

  • Liturgical celebrations and prayer with residents
  • Visiting the sick in the home or when hospitalised
  • Assisting in the kitchen and laundry departments
  • Helping with maintenance and general upkeep
  • Organising birthday party celebrations
  • Music, entertainment and recreational activities
  • IT support, administrative assistance
  • Sharing your own proposal or special talent

Religious Vocation

Called to become
a Little Sister

We are an international religious congregation of Pontifical rights, present in more than 30 countries. We have a unique charism of welcoming the elderly poor in our homes and caring for them as family until they return to their maker.

To become a Little Sister, you must be a practicing Catholic aged 18–25 with a C- or better. The journey of formation unfolds through several stages of discernment and growth.

Initial Observing

The aspirant comes to participate in daily life — prayer, hospitality and community — coming and going freely while discerning the call.

Aspirancy

A formal period where the young woman moves into the home to observe our life more deeply — lasting several months according to individual needs.

Pre-Postulancy — Benin, West Africa

As a French congregation, aspirants learn the language and are initiated into prayer, the spirit of the congregation, and its history.

Postulancy

One year of formal postulancy, deepening formation in the spirit and mission of Saint Jeanne Jugan.

Novitiate & First Vows

Two years of novitiate, culminating in first vows for three years and mission to one of our homes in service of the elderly.

The Four Vows

I

Chastity

A total gift of self to God and to those we serve, freeing us to love all without reserve.

II

Poverty

Sharing in the poverty of those we serve, trusting in Providence and the generosity of our benefactors for all our needs.

III

Obedience

Following the will of God as expressed through the Church, the congregation, and our superiors.

IV

Hospitality

Our unique 4th vow — the heart of our charism. To welcome the elderly poor as Christ, with unconditional joy and love.
